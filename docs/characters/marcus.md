# Marcus — Character Profile

## Basic Info

- **Name:** Marcus
- **Age:** Mid-to-late 30s (senior to the PC in company hierarchy, close in actual experience)
- **Role:** Senior engineer / tech lead at the same company. The person whose opinion matters in the room.
- **Appearance:** Not heavily described in existing scenes. What comes through: presence. He leans against counters. He looks at you directly. His posture is settled — a man comfortable taking up space. Physical details should be revealed gradually through the PC's increasing awareness of him.

## Personality

Marcus is direct. He asks what he means. He doesn't cushion questions or manage your reaction. When he says "what did you think," he wants what you think. This directness is the thing that gets under the PC's skin — in a professional world of hedged opinions and careful framing, his willingness to just ask is disarming.

**Speech patterns:**
- Direct questions without preamble. "What did you think of Kevin's deck?"
- Concise responses. "Yeah." "Fair." Three-line Slack messages that cover everything.
- When he disagrees, he provides data. He doesn't argue — he shows you the number that changes your read.
- When he agrees, it's confirmation, not compliment. A nod. "Yeah." Moving on.

**Professional style:**
- Earns respect by being right, not by demanding it
- Sends you work to review because he values your technical judgment (not as a favor)
- Doesn't explain things you already know (unlike Dan/Kevin)
- His "got 10 minutes?" is never 10 minutes and he knows it

## What He Represents

Marcus is the **"this wasn't supposed to happen"** register. The workplace tension thread. Not romance — proximity. Not courtship — collision. The attraction is situational, born from professional respect, mutual competence, and the enforced intimacy of working late in the same building.

The charge with Marcus comes from the fact that acting on it has *consequences*. Professional reputation, office dynamics, the career she rebuilt from scratch in this body. Jake is safe. Marcus is not safe. That's the point.

## Relationship Progression

1. **Work meeting** — First encounter. He's in the design review. Competent. Present. She notices him but the meeting is the scene.
2. **Break room coffee** — He asks her opinion directly. The directness is the inciting moment. She gives him a real answer and he receives it like that's normal.
3. **The spec review** — Asynchronous. Slack messages. Four exchanges that produce two updated positions. Professional intimacy through technical discourse.
4. **Working late** — Empty office. He's there too. The context change (night, empty building, no witnesses) shifts the frequency.
5. **After-work drinks** — He suggests it. Bar near the office. Two drinks in. The professional filter dissolves.
6. **The line crosses** — Office. Conference room or stairwell. Not a bedroom. The workplace setting is part of the charge.
7. **Monday morning** — What it looks like to walk into work after what happened.

## Key Dynamics

- **Mutual competence is the attraction.** He doesn't condescend (unlike Dan/Kevin). He sends her a 12-page spec because he wants her actual read. This recognition — being treated as an intellectual peer — is what triggers the physical response. The PC's former male self would have just appreciated the professional respect. This body adds a layer.
- **The professional context raises stakes.** Everything with Jake happens in private/social space. Everything with Marcus happens in or near the office. The risk of being seen. The career implications. The Monday-morning consequences.
- **He's not pursuing her.** The tension builds through proximity and circumstance, not intent. He didn't plan to work late the same night. He didn't plan for drinks to go that way. Neither of them did. That's what makes it "this wasn't supposed to happen."
- **He doesn't know about the transformation.** Same as Jake — he sees the woman at work. His respect is for her competence as she presents it.

## Trait Interactions

- **AMBITIOUS PC:** The career calculus is front and center. She's aware of what this could cost. She does it anyway (or doesn't).
- **ANALYTICAL PC:** She sees the entire trajectory from "what did you think of Kevin's deck" to "we should not be doing this." Seeing it doesn't stop it.
- **CONFIDENT PC:** Meets his directness with her own. The mutual frankness becomes charged.
- **SHY PC:** His directness is overwhelming. She can't deflect the way she does with everyone else. The vulnerability of being seen clearly.
- **OBJECTIFYING PC:** She recognizes the dynamics from the other side. Workplace attraction, power proximity, the calculation of consequences. She's run this playbook before, in different shoes.

## Voice Notes

When writing Marcus's dialogue:
- Economical. Professional even when personal.
- He concedes with data ("your number is better than mine") not with deference.
- Never flirty. The tension comes from what he doesn't say.
- His questions are real questions. He's not making conversation — he's acquiring information.
- Silence with Marcus isn't comfortable (that's Jake). Silence with Marcus is charged.
